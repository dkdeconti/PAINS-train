About

PAINS train is an attempt to use machine learning to identify false positives in chemical drug screens caused by pan assay interference compounds (PAINS). The basis for the PAINS classification is a 2010 paper by J.B. Baell1, in which PAINS were empirically derived from a large, diverse chemical screen.

More than 350 PAINS were identified and their structure was encoded in a SYBYL/SLN string. As a sample for the non-PAINS aspect of the training set, a random sampling (4,000 molecules) of chemicals from the ChEMBLdb version 20 was used.

RdKit was used for all SMILES, SDF, and SYBYL/SLN format translation, fingerprinting, and conversion of features to a bit array. A bit array of structural features was created for the training sets. A PCA of the two classifiers (PAINS and ChEMBL) showed a tight clustering of PAINS.


As a further control, I compared the STITCH version 4.0 chemical database of >82 million unique chemicals (randomly subsampled) to the PAINS set through PCA.

I chose a random forest classifier due to the features representation. The features were transformed Morgan fingerprints to a 2,048 length bit vector. As a bit vector, the lack of a continuous variable removes many clustering methods as options. This fact combined with the ambiguity as to the of dependence of points between each other in the vector made a random forest classifier an appropriate method for the classification.

Comparison of the performance of the random forest to knn reinforces the notion that random forest is a better performer.


I tested the random forest model against a cohort of FDA approved drugs and data regarding the number of side effects and the number of major gene expression changes. This data was obtained from DSigDB. I compared the probability prediciton of PAINS classification by the rando forest model to both the number of side effects and the number of major identified gene expression changes. There was no correlation with either factor, and no drugs were actually classified as a PAINS. However, above a certain probability of PAINS prediction there were no drugs with a low number of side effects.
